# Crash Course: Measure Theory, Convex Bodies, and the Minkowski Sum

**Phase 1 — Foundations**

> *"The essence of mathematics is not to make simple things complicated, but to make complicated things simple."*  
> — Stan Gudder

This document consolidates the essential mathematical prerequisites required for the rigorous study of convex geometry and the Brunn–Minkowski inequality. The material is organized into three interconnected pillars:

1. **Measure Theory**: Construction of the Lebesgue measure and slicing arguments via Fubini's theorem.
2. **Convex Bodies**: Formal definitions, compactness, and the support function in $\mathbb{R}^n$.
3. **The Minkowski Sum**: Geometric intuition on set addition and its regularizing properties.

---

## Part I — Measure Theory

### 1.1 Motivation: Why Measure Theory?

Classical analysis in $\mathbb{R}$ relies on the notion of *length* for intervals. However, extending this notion to arbitrary subsets of $\mathbb{R}^n$ encounters fundamental obstructions:

- **Vitali's construction** demonstrates that not every subset of $\mathbb{R}$ can be assigned a "length" in a translation-invariant and countably additive manner.
- The Banach–Tarski paradox further illustrates that naive "volume" assignments fail in higher dimensions.

The resolution lies in restricting attention to a sufficiently rich yet well-behaved collection of sets — the **$\sigma$-algebra** — on which a measure can be consistently defined.

### 1.2 $\sigma$-Algebras and Measurable Spaces

**Definition 1.2.1 ($\sigma$-algebra).** Let $E$ be a nonempty set. A collection $\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(E)$ is a **$\sigma$-algebra** (or **tribe**) on $E$ if:

1. $E \in \mathcal{A}$,
2. $A \in \mathcal{A} \Rightarrow A^c \in \mathcal{A}$,
3. If $(A_n)_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \subset \mathcal{A}$, then $\bigcup_{n \in \mathbb{N}} A_n \in \mathcal{A}$.

The pair $(E, \mathcal{A})$ is called a **measurable space**, and the elements of $\mathcal{A}$ are the **measurable sets**.

**Remarks.**

- Condition (3) combined with (2) yields stability under countable intersections.
- The $\sigma$-algebra captures the idea that "reasonable" operations (complements, countable unions) preserve measurability.

**Definition 1.2.2 (Generated $\sigma$-algebra).** For any collection $\mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(E)$, there exists a smallest $\sigma$-algebra containing $\mathcal{C}$, denoted $\sigma(\mathcal{C})$ and called the **$\sigma$-algebra generated by $\mathcal{C}$**:

$$
\sigma(\mathcal{C}) = \bigcap_{\substack{\mathcal{A} \text{ \(\sigma\)-algebra} \\ \mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{A}}} \mathcal{A}.
$$

**Definition 1.2.3 (Borel $\sigma$-algebra).** When $E$ is a topological space (e.g., $\mathbb{R}^n$ with the Euclidean topology), the **Borel $\sigma$-algebra** $\mathcal{B}(E)$ is defined as $\sigma(\mathcal{O})$, where $\mathcal{O}$ is the collection of all open sets. The elements of $\mathcal{B}(E)$ are called **Borel sets**.

For $\mathbb{R}$, one can verify that $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ is also generated by:

- Open intervals $(a,b)$,
- Half-lines $(-\infty, a)$,
- Half-lines with rational endpoints $(-\infty, q)$, $q \in \mathbb{Q}$.

**Definition 1.2.4 (Product $\sigma$-algebra).** Given measurable spaces $(E_1, \mathcal{A}_1)$ and $(E_2, \mathcal{A}_2)$, the **product $\sigma$-algebra** on $E_1 \times E_2$ is:

$$
\mathcal{A}_1 \otimes \mathcal{A}_2 := \sigma\bigl(\{A_1 \times A_2 : A_1 \in \mathcal{A}_1,\, A_2 \in \mathcal{A}_2\}\bigr).
$$

One can show that $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n) = \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}) \otimes \cdots \otimes \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ ($n$ times).

### 1.3 Positive Measures

**Definition 1.3.1 (Positive Measure).** Let $(E, \mathcal{A})$ be a measurable space. A function $\mu : \mathcal{A} \to [0, +\infty]$ is a **positive measure** if:

1. $\mu(\varnothing) = 0$,
2. **$\sigma$-additivity**: For any sequence $(A_n)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$ of pairwise disjoint sets in $\mathcal{A}$,

$$
\mu\Bigl(\bigcup_{n \in \mathbb{N}} A_n\Bigr) = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \mu(A_n).
$$

The triple $(E, \mathcal{A}, \mu)$ is called a **measure space**.

**Key Properties.**

1. **Monotonicity**: $A \subseteq B \Rightarrow \mu(A) \le \mu(B)$.
2. **Continuity from below**: If $A_n \uparrow A$ (increasing sequence with $A = \bigcup A_n$), then $\mu(A_n) \uparrow \mu(A)$.
3. **Continuity from above**: If $B_n \downarrow B$ (decreasing) and $\mu(B_0) < \infty$, then $\mu(B_n) \downarrow \mu(B)$.
4. **Subadditivity**: $\mu\bigl(\bigcup A_n\bigr) \le \sum \mu(A_n)$.

**Terminology.**

- $\mu$ is **finite** if $\mu(E) < \infty$.
- $\mu$ is a **probability measure** if $\mu(E) = 1$.
- $\mu$ is **$\sigma$-finite** if $E = \bigcup_{n} E_n$ with $\mu(E_n) < \infty$ for all $n$.

### 1.4 Construction of the Lebesgue Measure

The Lebesgue measure on $\mathbb{R}^n$ is the canonical measure extending the intuitive notion of "volume."

#### 1.4.1 Outer Measures

**Definition 1.4.1 (Outer Measure).** An **outer measure** on $E$ is a function $\mu^* : \mathcal{P}(E) \to [0, +\infty]$ satisfying:

1. $\mu^*(\varnothing) = 0$,
2. **Monotonicity**: $A \subseteq B \Rightarrow \mu^*(A) \le \mu^*(B)$,
3. **Countable subadditivity**: $\mu^*\bigl(\bigcup_n A_n\bigr) \le \sum_n \mu^*(A_n)$.

**Intuition.** An outer measure provides an "overestimate" of volume defined on *all* subsets of $E$, but it is generally not additive. The key insight is to identify a subcollection of sets on which $\mu^*$ *is* additive.

#### 1.4.2 The Carathéodory Construction

**Theorem 1.4.2 (Carathéodory).** Let $\mu^*$ be an outer measure on $E$. Define a set $A \subseteq E$ to be **$\mu^*$-measurable** if for every $B \subseteq E$:

$$
\mu^*(B) = \mu^*(B \cap A) + \mu^*(B \cap A^c).
$$

Then:

1. The collection $\mathcal{M}(\mu^*)$ of $\mu^*$-measurable sets is a $\sigma$-algebra.
2. The restriction $\mu := \mu^*|_{\mathcal{M}(\mu^*)}$ is a complete measure.

**Remark.** The Carathéodory criterion extracts the "well-behaved" sets from the perspective of additivity.

#### 1.4.3 Definition of the Lebesgue Outer Measure

For $\mathbb{R}^n$, define the **Lebesgue outer measure** by covering with open rectangles (or cubes). For $n=1$:

$$
\lambda^*(A) := \inf\Bigl\{\sum_{k=1}^\infty (b_k - a_k) : A \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty (a_k, b_k)\Bigr\}.
$$

**Properties of $\lambda^*$:**

- For any interval $I = [a, b]$: $\lambda^*(I) = b - a$.
- $\lambda^*$ is translation-invariant: $\lambda^*(A + x) = \lambda^*(A)$.

**Theorem 1.4.3 (Existence and Uniqueness of Lebesgue Measure).** There exists a unique measure $\lambda$ on $(\mathbb{R}^n, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n))$ such that for every open rectangle $R = (a_1, b_1) \times \cdots \times (a_n, b_n)$:

$$
\lambda(R) = \prod_{i=1}^n (b_i - a_i).
$$

This measure $\lambda$ is the **Lebesgue measure**.

**Proof Sketch (Uniqueness via Monotone Class Lemma).** The class of open rectangles is stable under finite intersections and generates $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n)$. The Monotone Class Theorem then implies that any two $\sigma$-finite measures agreeing on rectangles must coincide on all Borel sets.

### 1.5 Integration and the Theorems of Convergence

Once the Lebesgue measure is established, integration proceeds via:

1. **Simple (step) functions**: $f = \sum_{i=1}^k \alpha_i \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$, with $\int f\, d\mu = \sum \alpha_i \mu(A_i)$.
2. **Nonnegative measurable functions**: $\int f\, d\mu = \sup\bigl\{\int h\, d\mu : h \text{ simple}, 0 \le h \le f\bigr\}$.
3. **General integrable functions**: $f = f^+ - f^-$, with $\int f\, d\mu = \int f^+\, d\mu - \int f^-\, d\mu$ (when at least one is finite).

**Theorem 1.5.1 (Monotone Convergence Theorem).** Let $(f_n)$ be a sequence of nonnegative measurable functions with $f_n \uparrow f$. Then:

$$
\int f\, d\mu = \lim_{n \to \infty} \int f_n\, d\mu.
$$

**Theorem 1.5.2 (Fatou's Lemma).** For any sequence of nonnegative measurable functions:

$$
\int \liminf_{n \to \infty} f_n\, d\mu \le \liminf_{n \to \infty} \int f_n\, d\mu.
$$

**Theorem 1.5.3 (Dominated Convergence Theorem).** Suppose $f_n \to f$ a.e., and there exists an integrable function $g$ such that $|f_n| \le g$ a.e. for all $n$. Then $f$ is integrable and:

$$
\lim_{n \to \infty} \int f_n\, d\mu = \int f\, d\mu.
$$

### 1.6 Product Measures and Fubini's Theorem

#### 1.6.1 Construction of Product Measures

**Theorem 1.6.1.** Let $(E_1, \mathcal{A}_1, \mu_1)$ and $(E_2, \mathcal{A}_2, \mu_2)$ be $\sigma$-finite measure spaces. There exists a unique measure $\mu_1 \otimes \mu_2$ on $(E_1 \times E_2, \mathcal{A}_1 \otimes \mathcal{A}_2)$ such that:

$$
(\mu_1 \otimes \mu_2)(A_1 \times A_2) = \mu_1(A_1) \cdot \mu_2(A_2)
$$

for all $A_1 \in \mathcal{A}_1$, $A_2 \in \mathcal{A}_2$.

This measure is called the **product measure**.

#### 1.6.2 Fubini–Tonelli Theorem

**Theorem 1.6.2 (Fubini–Tonelli).** Let $f : E_1 \times E_2 \to [0, +\infty]$ be measurable with respect to $\mathcal{A}_1 \otimes \mathcal{A}_2$. Then:

$$
\int_{E_1 \times E_2} f\, d(\mu_1 \otimes \mu_2) = \int_{E_1} \Bigl(\int_{E_2} f(x, y)\, d\mu_2(y)\Bigr)\, d\mu_1(x)
= \int_{E_2} \Bigl(\int_{E_1} f(x, y)\, d\mu_1(x)\Bigr)\, d\mu_2(y).
$$

For functions $f$ (possibly signed or complex) that are integrable with respect to the product measure, the equality of iterated integrals holds as well.

**Intuition.** Fubini's theorem justifies "slicing" a higher-dimensional integral into iterated lower-dimensional integrals. This is fundamental in convex geometry, where one computes volumes of convex bodies via slicing (e.g., the Cavalieri principle).

#### 1.6.3 Application: Volume of the Unit Ball

Using polar coordinates and Fubini's theorem, the volume of the Euclidean unit ball $B^n \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ is:

$$
\text{vol}(B^n) = \frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma\bigl(\tfrac{n}{2} + 1\bigr)}.
$$

**Sketch.** Write $B^n = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^n : \|x\|_2 \le 1\}$. By Fubini:

$$
\text{vol}(B^n) = \int_{-1}^{1} \text{vol}_{n-1}\bigl(B^{n-1}(\sqrt{1 - x_n^2})\bigr)\, dx_n.
$$

The recurrence, combined with the Gamma function, yields the closed form.

## Part II — The Theory of Convex Bodies

### 2.1 Fundamental Definitions

Throughout, we work in $\mathbb{R}^n$ endowed with the Euclidean inner product $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ and norm $\|\cdot\|$.

**Definition 2.1.1 (Convex Set).** A set $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is **convex** if for all $x, y \in A$ and $\alpha \in [0, 1]$:

$$
\alpha x + (1 - \alpha) y \in A.
$$

Geometrically, $A$ contains the line segment joining any two of its points.

**Examples.**

- The empty set $\varnothing$ and the whole space $\mathbb{R}^n$ are convex.
- Half-spaces $\{x : \langle u, x \rangle \le c\}$ are convex.
- Euclidean balls $B(x_0, r) = \{x : \|x - x_0\| \le r\}$ are convex.
- Affine subspaces and polyhedra (finite intersections of half-spaces) are convex.

**Proposition 2.1.2.** A set $A$ is convex if and only if it contains all convex combinations of its elements:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^k \alpha_i x_i \in A \quad \text{whenever } x_1, \ldots, x_k \in A,\; \alpha_i \ge 0,\; \sum_i \alpha_i = 1.
$$

**Definition 2.1.3 (Convex Hull).** For any set $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$, the **convex hull** $\text{conv}(A)$ is the smallest convex set containing $A$:

$$
\text{conv}(A) = \Bigl\{\sum_{i=1}^k \alpha_i x_i : k \in \mathbb{N},\; x_i \in A,\; \alpha_i \ge 0,\; \sum_i \alpha_i = 1\Bigr\}.
$$

**Theorem 2.1.4 (Carathéodory).** If $x \in \text{conv}(A)$ for some $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$, then $x$ can be expressed as a convex combination of at most $n+1$ points from $A$.

**Intuition.** In dimension $n$, every point in the convex hull is contained in some simplex with vertices in $A$.

---

### 2.2 Convex Bodies: Compactness and Topological Properties

**Definition 2.2.1 (Convex Body).** A **convex body** in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a nonempty, compact, convex subset $K \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$.

**Remark.** Some authors require convex bodies to have nonempty interior. We follow the convention that a convex body may be lower-dimensional; when we need $\text{int}(K) \ne \varnothing$, this will be stated explicitly.

**Proposition 2.2.2 (Interior of Convex Sets).** Every nonempty convex set $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ has nonempty **relative interior** (interior with respect to its affine hull).

**Proposition 2.2.3 (Topological Properties of Convex Sets).** If $A$ is convex, then:

1. $\text{cl}(A)$ is convex.
2. $\text{int}(A)$ is convex (if nonempty).
3. $\text{cl}(\text{int}(A)) = \text{cl}(A)$ and $\text{int}(\text{cl}(A)) = \text{int}(A)$ (when $\text{int}(A) \ne \varnothing$).

**Theorem 2.2.4 (Convex Hull of Compact Sets).** If $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is compact, then $\text{conv}(A)$ is compact.

---

### 2.3 Separation and Support Theorems

The interplay between convex sets and linear functionals is captured by separation theorems.

**Theorem 2.3.1 (Supporting Hyperplane Theorem).** Let $K \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ be a closed convex set. Through every boundary point $x \in \partial K$, there exists a **supporting hyperplane**: a hyperplane $H = \{y : \langle u, y \rangle = c\}$ with $x \in H$ and $K \subseteq \{y : \langle u, y \rangle \le c\}$.

**Theorem 2.3.2 (Separation Theorem).** Let $K, L \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ be nonempty, disjoint, closed, convex sets with at least one compact. Then there exists a hyperplane **strictly separating** $K$ and $L$: for some $u \ne 0$ and $c_1 < c_2$,

$$
\sup_{x \in K} \langle u, x \rangle \le c_1 < c_2 \le \inf_{y \in L} \langle u, y \rangle.
$$

**Corollary 2.3.3.** A closed convex set $K \ne \mathbb{R}^n$ equals the intersection of all its supporting half-spaces.

---

### 2.4 The Support Function

One of the most powerful tools in convex geometry is the **support function**, which provides a dual description of convex bodies.

**Definition 2.4.1 (Support Function).** For a nonempty compact convex set $K \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$, the **support function** $h_K : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is:

$$
h_K(u) := \max_{x \in K} \langle u, x \rangle.
$$

**Geometric Interpretation.** For a unit vector $u \in S^{n-1}$, $h_K(u)$ is the signed distance from the origin to the supporting hyperplane of $K$ with outer normal $u$.

**Theorem 2.4.2 (Properties of the Support Function).** Let $K, L$ be convex bodies in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Then:

1. **Positive homogeneity**: $h_K(\alpha u) = \alpha\, h_K(u)$ for $\alpha \ge 0$.
2. **Subadditivity**: $h_K(u + v) \le h_K(u) + h_K(v)$.
3. Hence $h_K$ is **convex** (as a function on $\mathbb{R}^n$).
4. **Translation**: $h_{K + a}(u) = h_K(u) + \langle u, a \rangle$.
5. **Scalar multiplication**: $h_{\alpha K}(u) = \alpha\, h_K(u)$ for $\alpha \ge 0$.
6. **Minkowski sum**: $h_{K+L}(u) = h_K(u) + h_L(u)$.
7. **Uniqueness**: $K = L \iff h_K = h_L$.

**Intuition.** The support function encodes the geometry of $K$ through linear functionals. Operations on convex bodies (translations, sums, dilations) translate into simple algebraic operations on support functions. This linearization is a key reason the support function is central to convex geometry.

**Examples.**

- **Singleton** $K = \{a\}$: $h_K(u) = \langle u, a \rangle$.
- **Euclidean ball** $B^n = \{x : \|x\| \le 1\}$: $h_{B^n}(u) = \|u\|$.
- **Segment** $[-a, a]$ (through origin): $h(u) = |\langle u, a \rangle|$.
- **Polytope** $P = \text{conv}\{v_1, \ldots, v_k\}$: $h_P(u) = \max_{1 \le i \le k} \langle u, v_i \rangle$.

## Part III — The Minkowski Sum

### 3.1 Definition and Basic Properties

**Definition 3.1.1 (Minkowski Sum).** For subsets $A, B \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$, the **Minkowski sum** is:

$$
A + B := \{a + b : a \in A,\, b \in B\}.
$$

More generally, for $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$
\alpha A + \beta B := \{\alpha a + \beta b : a \in A,\, b \in B\}.
$$

**Special Cases.**

- $A + \{x\}$ is the translate of $A$ by vector $x$.
- $\alpha A$ is the **dilation** (or scaling) of $A$ by factor $\alpha$.
- $-A := (-1) A$ is the **reflection** of $A$ about the origin.
- $A - B := A + (-B)$.

**Proposition 3.1.2 (Basic Properties).** Let $A, B, C \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$. Then:

1. **Commutativity**: $A + B = B + A$.
2. **Associativity**: $(A + B) + C = A + (B + C)$.
3. **Preservation of convexity**: If $A$ and $B$ are convex, so is $A + B$.
4. **Distributivity for convex sets**: If $A$ is convex and $\alpha, \beta \ge 0$, then $\alpha A + \beta A = (\alpha + \beta) A$.

**Remark.** In general, $A + A \ne 2A$ and $A - A \ne \{0\}$. For instance, if $A = \{0, 1\}$, then $A + A = \{0, 1, 2\}$, while $2A = \{0, 2\}$.

---

### 3.2 Geometric Intuition: Union vs. Minkowski Sum

A natural question arises: how does the Minkowski sum differ from the union?

| **Operation**        | **Definition**                                   | **Convexity preserved?** | **Volume growth** |
|----------------------|--------------------------------------------------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| Union $A \cup B$     | Set-theoretic union                              | No (in general)          | $\max$-like       |
| Minkowski $A + B$    | Pointwise vector addition                        | Yes (if $A$, $B$ convex) | Polynomial        |

- The **union** merges the sets, potentially introducing non-convexity.
- The **Minkowski sum** "sweeps" one set over the other, preserving and often enhancing regularity.

---

### 3.3 The Smoothing Effect of the Minkowski Sum

One of the most remarkable properties of the Minkowski sum is its **regularizing** (smoothing) effect:

> **Adding a "round" set to a "sharp" set smooths the corners.**

**Theorem 3.3.1 (Smoothing via Ball Addition).** Let $K$ be a convex body and let $B_r = rB^n$ denote the closed ball of radius $r > 0$. The **outer parallel body** (or $r$-offset) of $K$ is:

$$
K_r := K + B_r = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^n : d(x, K) \le r\},
$$

where $d(x, K) = \inf_{y \in K} \|x - y\|$.

**Properties of $K_r$:**

1. $K_r$ is convex.
2. If $K$ is a polytope with vertices and edges, $K_r$ has **rounded corners** and **smooth edges**.
3. The boundary $\partial K_r$ is of class $C^{1,1}$ (Lipschitz normal) whenever $r > 0$.

**Intuition.** Adding a ball "rounds off" the singularities (corners, edges) of the original body. The smaller the ball, the more localized the smoothing; the larger the ball, the more the body tends toward a ball.

**Example 3.3.2 (Square + Disk).** Let $S = [-1, 1]^2 \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ be the unit square, and $D_r = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^2 : \|x\| \le r\}$ the disk of radius $r$. Then:

$$
S + D_r = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^2 : d(x, S) \le r\}.
$$

Geometrically:

- The four edges of the square are "pushed outward" by distance $r$, remaining straight.
- The four corners are replaced by quarter-circles of radius $r$.

The resulting shape is a **rounded rectangle** (or **stadium** in the degenerate case).

---

### 3.4 Support Function and the Minkowski Sum

The support function provides an elegant algebraic characterization of the Minkowski sum:

**Theorem 3.4.1.** Let $K, L$ be convex bodies. Then:

$$
h_{K+L}(u) = h_K(u) + h_L(u) \quad \text{for all } u \in \mathbb{R}^n.
$$

**Proof.**

$$
h_{K+L}(u) = \max_{z \in K + L} \langle u, z \rangle
= \max_{x \in K,\, y \in L} \langle u, x + y \rangle
= \max_{x \in K} \langle u, x \rangle + \max_{y \in L} \langle u, y \rangle
= h_K(u) + h_L(u).
$$

**Corollary 3.4.2.** For the ball $B^n$:

$$
h_{K + rB^n}(u) = h_K(u) + r\|u\|.
$$

Restricted to the unit sphere $S^{n-1}$, this becomes $h_{K + rB^n}(u) = h_K(u) + r$.

---

### 3.5 Steiner's Formula: Volume of Parallel Bodies

The volume of the parallel body $K + rB^n$ is a polynomial in $r$, a fundamental result due to Steiner.

**Theorem 3.5.1 (Steiner's Formula).** Let $K \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ be a convex body. Then:

$$
\text{vol}_n(K + rB^n) = \sum_{i=0}^{n} \binom{n}{i} W_i(K)\, r^i,
$$

where $W_0(K), W_1(K), \ldots, W_n(K)$ are the **quermassintegrals** (or intrinsic volumes) of $K$.

**Special cases:**

- $W_0(K) = \text{vol}_n(K)$,
- $W_1(K) = \tfrac{1}{n} S(K)$, where $S(K)$ is the surface area,
- $W_n(K) = \text{vol}_n(B^n)$ (independent of $K$).

**Example ($n = 2$).** For a convex body $K \subset \mathbb{R}^2$:

$$
\text{area}(K + rD) = \text{area}(K) + r \cdot \text{perimeter}(K) + \pi r^2.
$$

For the square $S = [-1,1]^2$:

$$
\text{area}(S + D_r) = 4 + 8r + \pi r^2.
$$

---

### 3.6 Why the Minkowski Sum Matters

The Minkowski sum is ubiquitous in:

1. **The Brunn–Minkowski Inequality**: The central inequality relating volumes of convex bodies under Minkowski addition:

   $$
   \text{vol}(K + L)^{1/n} \ge \text{vol}(K)^{1/n} + \text{vol}(L)^{1/n}.
   $$

   Equality holds if and only if $K$ and $L$ are homothetic.

2. **Isoperimetric Inequalities**: The classical isoperimetric inequality is a special case of Brunn–Minkowski (taking $L = \varepsilon B^n$ and differentiating).

3. **Mixed Volumes**: The polynomial expansion of $\text{vol}(\lambda K + \mu L)$ leads to mixed volumes, a central object in convex geometry.

4. **High-Dimensional Probability**: Concentration inequalities (e.g., Talagrand's inequality) exploit the geometry of Minkowski sums.

5. **Computational Geometry and Robotics**: Minkowski sums characterize configuration-space obstacles in motion planning.
